Most Shared 3D Printing Models? Printer Upgrades

Data from a top 3D model sharing site highlights the technology's limitations, as most models focus on upgrading printers rather than other applications.

3D printing technologies emerged with an exhilarating promise straight out of science fiction: not only could people design and produce exactly what they needed from the comfort of their homes, but they could also bypass traditional shopping altogether.

This revolutionary layer-by-layer innovation empowered individuals to control the means of production, democratizing access to machines capable of creating factory-quality objects without the oversight of capitalist enterprises.

The reality, however, is much more limited. Data shows that most 3D printer owners spend their time trying to make their printers work better.

The limited use of personal 3D printing can vary, but, in a nutshell, the technology remains challenging. Consumers often find themselves frustrated by the effort required to print an object without errors, and the high bar for designing objects can be prohibitive for many users. While the printers are cute and affordable, they are not as user-friendly in reality.

"Cute Mini Octopus" by McGybeer is one of the most free popular models, with 112,535 downloads.

"Democratization in the Workplace"

As usual, the industry adapts to meet demand. Interestingly, the focus of democratization has shifted from individual users to businesses, once seen as the main adversaries of the 3D printing movement. Forbes reports, "In the next few years, we’ll be seeing more democratization of 3D printing in the workplace [...] anyone in an office should be able to learn 3D printing quickly and easily, then fabricate models like they’re printing copies on paper."

"Prusa Face Shield"

The promise of the 3D printing movement gained renewed attention during the COVID-19 pandemic. Within just three days, 3D Printers Prusa developed a face shield that became the second most downloaded object, amassing 218,045 downloads since May 2020. Amid supply chain disruptions, lockdowns, and stringent social distancing measures, 3D printers enabled individuals to produce parts of face masks independently.

However, it is crucial to note that the printed parts required assembly with additional components, such as the face shield, which had to be purchased separately to complete the face protector. While this approach perhaps missed the main point of fully independent production, it provided a glimpse of the technology's potential.

Part of "Prusa Face Shield" by Prusa is the second most downloaded printable model, with 218,046 downloads.

In other, more specialized fields, 3D printers are part of a significant shift in manufacturing, particularly in healthcare. Advanced 3D printers can now work with organic materials, enabling the printing of biological tissues, which has enormous potential for regenerative medicine and organ transplantation.
